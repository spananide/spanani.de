Evaluating Quality

Professionals use the B.L.I.C. framework to objectively rate a wine's quality, regardless of personal preference.

Balance

The harmony between sweetness, acidity, tannin, and alcohol. No single element should stick out aggressively.

Length

Also known as the "finish." How long does the flavor linger on your palate after swallowing? The longer, the better.

Intensity

How concentrated are the flavors? A high-quality wine will have rich, clearly defined aromas that jump out of the glass.

Complexity

Does the wine have multiple layers? A great wine evolves in the glass, offering primary fruit, secondary oak, and tertiary age notes.

The Standard Tasting Process

Master the essential steps of wine evaluation.

1 See & Swirl

First, tilt the glass over a white background to check the color and clarity. Is it pale ruby or deep garnet? Then, place the glass on a table and swirl it gently. This coats the sides of the glass, increasing the surface area and allowing oxygen to release the wine's volatile aromatic compounds.

2 Sniff

Don't be shy—stick your nose directly into the glass and take a short, deep sniff. Look for primary aromas (fruit, floral notes), secondary aromas (vanilla, toast from oak barrels), and tertiary aromas (leather, mushroom from aging). Trust your first instinct.

3 Sip & Savor

Take a slightly larger sip than normal and swish it around to coat your entire mouth. Draw in a little air over the wine to further aerate it on your palate. Pay attention to the texture (tannin), the mouth-watering effect (acidity), and finally, evaluate the length of the finish.
